Nursing facilities near Kerrville, TX — for rehab + long-term bariatric care

For a resident who has recently lost the ability to walk (weight-related deconditioning), needs heavy hands-on care now, has a rehab goal (rebuild enough strength to transfer / walk to the bathroom), is cognitively intact, and may stay long-term (years) on Medicaid. What "rehab + walk to the bathroom" actually requires (read first)

Honest framing: regaining ambulation in someone who recently became non-ambulatory because of weight is possible but not guaranteed — it depends heavily on whether the loss is deconditioning (reversible with sustained therapy) versus a fixed cause, and on the weight itself. A nursing facility can deliver the therapy; whether the patient walks to the bathroom again depends on the body and the consistency of the program. What to insist on when you call:

  • PT/OT 5+ days a week, with a written plan toward sit-to-stand, transfers, and short supervised ambulation — not a token evaluation. Ask the therapy-staffing ratio and whether therapy is in-house or a contract company.
  • A restorative-nursing program. Formal Medicare-covered PT runs ~weeks; after it ends, gains evaporate unless CNAs continue a daily walking/standing program. Ask: "When skilled therapy ends, who continues the walking program, and how often?" This is the single biggest predictor of whether a bathroom-walking goal survives past month two.
  • Bariatric-rated rehab equipment — parallel bars, gait belt, sit-to-stand lift, and a bariatric wheelchair all rated to the patient's weight. Standard gym equipment won't bear it.
  • Enough hands. Heavy-care repositioning + transfer training needs 2–3 trained staff per transfer, every shift. The Nurse hrs/day and Staffing columns below are your proxy; below ~3.4 is thin for this acuity.
  • Skin/wound prevention. A newly immobile heavy patient is at high pressure-injury risk in the gap before mobility returns — ask about repositioning schedule and a weight-rated pressure-redistribution mattress.

Tension to weigh: facilities branded "Nursing and Rehabilitation" are often oriented to short Medicare rehab stays and discharge. You want one that does strong therapy and will retain a long-term Medicaid resident. Confirm both explicitly — don't assume.

Where to read high-quality reviews — Texas sources

Same hierarchy as anywhere: government data tells you which homes are safe; consumer reviews tell you what daily life feels like but are gameable; bariatric + rehab capability is in no database and must be confirmed by phone and a visit.

Federal (the gold standard — use first)

  • Medicare Care Compare — medicare.gov/care-compare. Read the three component stars separately (health inspection = most trustworthy; staffing = payroll-verified; quality measures = self-reported, weakest). For this patient, read both the short-stay rehab measures (improvement in function/mobility) and the long-stay measures (pressure ulcers, falls, weight loss). Check Special-Focus status and avoid any SFF or SFF candidate.
  • ProPublica Nursing Home Inspect — projects.propublica.org/nursing-homes. Searchable inspection narratives, fines, serious deficiencies. Read what actually happened.
  • US News Best Nursing Homes — health.usnews.com/best-nursing-homes. It rates Short-Term Rehabilitation and Long-Term Care separately — useful here because you care about both. Derived from CMS data, so a cross-check, not independent signal.

Texas-specific (adds state-licensure detail the federal tools miss)

  • Texas HHS Long-Term Care Provider Search — apps.hhs.texas.gov/ltcsearch. Search by city/county/ZIP; each profile lists inspection violations grouped into Health and Life Safety Code, with dates and correction dates, plus whether the facility takes Medicare/Medicaid and is Alzheimer's-certified. The Texas counterpart to Care Compare.
  • TULIP public search — tulip.hhs.texas.gov. The Texas licensure portal: full inspection reports, deficiency citations, complaints, and enforcement actions, no login.
  • There is no separate Texas consumer star rating. When you see "stars" on a Texas home, those are the federal CMS Five-Star — Texas defers to it. The state programs (Quality Monitoring Program, Minimum Performance Standards, QIPP) are provider/payment-facing and use CMS stars as their yardstick. So: the Texas value-add is the violation detail in the two portals above, not a parallel score.
  • To report a problem or request records: HHSC Complaint & Incident Intake — 1-800-458-9858. Facilities must also show survey reports on-site on request.

The ombudsman — free, conflict-free, and the best-kept secret

A resident-side advocate with no financial stake. Call before placing to ask candidly which local homes generate the most complaints and which actually have the lift equipment and staffing for an immobile bariatric resident; call after placing to resolve care, discharge, or billing disputes without you having to confront the facility alone.

  • Texas State LTC Ombudsman — ltco.texas.gov · 1-800-252-2412.
  • Local program for all three counties: conveniently, Kerr, Gillespie, and Kendall all fall under one agency — the Alamo Area Agency on Aging (AACOG). Regional line (866) 231-4922; ombudsman program direct (210) 218-7306. aacog.gov.
  • 2-1-1 Texas — dial 2-1-1 (or 877-541-7905), 24/7, for referrals and benefits help. AACOG also offers free benefits counseling on the Medicaid nursing-facility pathway.

Texas Medicaid for a multi-year stay — two things to handle early

  • STAR+PLUS (hhs.texas.gov) — Texas delivers long-term nursing-facility Medicaid through this managed-care program. Qualifying requires a Nursing Facility Level of Care determination (MN/LOC assessment). The recent loss of ambulation and heavy-care needs make the level-of-care case straightforward, but the resident will be enrolled with a STAR+PLUS plan that manages the benefit.
  • Medicaid Estate Recovery (MERP) (hhs.texas.gov) — after death, Texas can claim against the estate to recover long-term-care costs for recipients 55+. The home is exempt while the recipient lives but is not automatically protected afterward. For a 5-year spend-down, get an elder-law attorney involved early — exemptions, hardship waivers, and transfer timing materially change the outcome.

Consumer reviews — texture only, never the deciding factor

Google Maps is the highest-volume source; read the 3-star reviews (most honest) and look for recurring specifics (call-lights, smell, lost laundry, missed therapy), not the average. Avoid letting "A Place for Mom," SeniorAdvisor, or Caring "advisors" narrow your list — they're paid-referral channels that only show homes that pay them, and a form submission triggers a barrage of sales calls. Nursing-home star averages are unreliable anyway (tiny samples; a barbell of grieving families and staff-solicited 5-stars). Triangulate the inspection narrative + staffing data + what current long-term families and the ombudsman tell you.

Phone screen — ask every candidate (rehab + bariatric)

  1. "My relative recently became unable to walk. What can your therapy team realistically do toward transfers and walking to the bathroom — PT/OT how many days a week, in-house or contracted?"
  2. "After skilled therapy ends, do you have a restorative-nursing program that continues a daily walking/standing routine? Who runs it and how often?"
  3. "Do you currently care for bariatric residents over [weight]? How many right now?"
  4. "What's the rated weight capacity — in pounds — of your beds, ceiling/floor lifts, slings, wheelchairs, shower chairs, and rehab gym equipment (parallel bars, sit-to-stand)?" (Beds ~1.5× the resident's weight.)
  5. "How many trained staff perform a bariatric transfer, on every shift including nights and weekends?"
  6. "Can the bathroom/shower physically fit a bariatric shower chair and a lift?"
  7. "Pressure-injury prevention while mobility is limited — repositioning schedule, mattress type and its weight rating, who manages wound care?"
  8. "Are you willing and able to keep my relative long-term on Medicaid after any rehab benefit ends? Do you have Medicaid-certified beds available now?"
  9. "Any bariatric-handling injuries (resident or staff) in the past year?"

If a facility is vague on rated capacities, on round-the-clock transfer staffing, or on continuing therapy after the Medicare benefit lapses, cross it off regardless of its star rating. Then visit twice — once unannounced on an evening or weekend, when staffing is thinnest — and ask current long-term families in the hallway what they'd change.
