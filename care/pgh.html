How the filtering works

  • Medicaid is mandatory. A 5-year private-pay stay is ~$700k–$900k; almost everyone spends down to Medicaid. Facilities that don't take Medicaid for long-term care are disqualified regardless of quality — this knocks out the three best-rated homes on the list (Harmony, Longwood at Oakmont, UPMC Magee TCU).
  • CMS overall star is a floor, not the decision. Read the Health-inspection and Staffing component stars separately — overall stars can hide a bad inspection record. Any abuse flag or immediate-jeopardy (J/K/L) citation in the last ~2 years is treated as disqualifying.
  • Nurse hrs/day is the raw figure from the PA table (total nurse hours per resident per day). For an immobile bariatric resident needing round-the-clock repositioning and lift transfers, more is better; below ~3.5 is thin.
  • Bariatric capability is in NO database. Only one facility (Whitehall) has even a third-party mention of bariatric beds. This must be confirmed by phone + tour for every candidate (see checklist at bottom).

Where to read high-quality reviews — the source landscape

Objective government data tells you which homes are safe; consumer reviews tell you what daily life feels like but are easily gamed; bariatric capability is in no database. Use all three, weighted in that order.

Objective / clinical (the gold standard)

  • Medicare Care Compare — medicare.gov/care-compare. The single best tool. Read the three components separately: health inspections (hardest to game — trust most), staffing (payroll-verified RN hours + turnover), and quality measures (self-reported — weakest). For this resident, read long-stay measures: pressure ulcers/bedsores, falls with injury, UTIs, weight loss, antipsychotic use. Check for Special Focus Facility status and avoid any SFF.
  • ProPublica "Nursing Home Inspect" — projects.propublica.org/nursing-homes. Full-text-searchable inspection narratives, fines, and serious deficiencies. Read the story of what went wrong, not just the star.
  • PA Dept. of Health surveys — the locator you used + the scope & severity guide. Deficiencies run A (minor) → L (most serious). Scattered D-level findings are normal; any G or higher (actual harm), and especially J/K/L (immediate jeopardy), is a red flag — disqualifying if it involves pressure ulcers, falls, or transfer/lift failures.
  • CMS Payroll-Based Journal — data.cms.gov. Raw, auditable staffing. Watch weekend/night staffing — that's when an immobile bariatric resident misses repositioning and pressure injuries start.
  • US News Best Nursing Homes — health.usnews.com/best-nursing-homes. Filter to Long-Term Care. Convenient, but derived from the same CMS data — a cross-check, not independent signal.
  • PA Long-Term Care Ombudsman — 717-783-8975 · request page. A free, resident-side advocate with no financial stake. Ask which of your finalists generate the most complaints. The only conflict-free "review" source, and the most underused.

Consumer / experiential (useful texture, heavily biased)

  • Google Maps reviews — highest volume. Read the 3-star reviews (most honest) and look for recurring specifics (call lights, smell, lost laundry), not the average.
  • Caring.com — directory + reviews, but also a paid-referral business; ignore its recommendations, treat reviews like Google's.
  • A Place for Mom / SeniorAdvisor.com / referral "advisors" — paid-referral conflict of interest. Free to you because the facility pays them a commission (often the resident's first month, up to ~$12k). They only show facilities that pay them; a genuinely good non-participating home never appears. Fine as a directory; never let an "advisor" narrow your list. Expect sales calls the moment you submit a form.
  • Yelp / Facebook / Reddit (r/AgingParents, r/pittsburgh) — low volume, weak correlation with CMS quality. Occasional vivid specifics worth a follow-up call; not data.

Why consumer star averages are unreliable here: tiny sample sizes (one review swings it), bimodal bias (grieving families of deceased residents vs. staff-solicited 5-stars — a barbell, not a verdict), and studies find social-media ratings correlate only weakly with CMS measures. Triangulate the inspection narrative + staffing data + what current long-term families and the ombudsman tell you instead.

Bariatric capability — confirm by phone + tour (no website has this)

Pressure injuries, dropped-transfer injuries, and staff back-injuries (which drive turnover) are the predictable failure modes when a facility takes a bariatric resident it isn't equipped for. Ask every candidate:

  1. "Do you currently care for bariatric residents over [weight]? How many right now?"
  2. "What's the rated weight capacity — in pounds — of your beds, ceiling/floor lifts, slings, wheelchairs, and shower chairs?" (Beds should be rated to ~1.5× the resident's weight.)
  3. "Ceiling lifts or floor lifts only? Is the assigned room equipped with one?"
  4. "Do you have a formal safe-patient-handling / no-manual-lift policy? How many trained staff perform a bariatric transfer, on every shift including nights and weekends?"
  5. "Can the bathroom/shower physically fit a bariatric shower chair and a lift?"
  6. "Pressure-injury prevention protocol — repositioning schedule, mattress type and its weight rating, who manages wound care?"
  7. "How does an immobile bariatric resident get out of the room and outdoors, and how often?"
  8. "Any bariatric-handling injuries (resident or staff) in the past year?"

If a facility is vague on rated capacities or can't confirm round-the-clock trained transfer staffing, cross it off regardless of its star rating.

How to actually decide, for a 5-year stay by a mentally-intact resident

  1. Shortlist from objective data (this table → Tier A), drop anything with weak inspection/staffing component stars or any SFF status, cross-check on ProPublica.
  2. Phone-screen for bariatric capability before touring — most will not pass cleanly.
  3. Visit twice, once unannounced on an evening/weekend when staffing is thinnest. Use your senses: persistent urine odor = a staffing failure; check call-light response, whether residents are dressed/groomed/out of bed, and whether staff use residents' names.
  4. Talk to current long-term families in the hallway/dining room (not the ones marketing introduces). Ask the Resident Council and to read the most recent survey binder (your legal right). Call the Allegheny ombudsman.
  5. Weight quality-of-LIFE heavily — for a cognitively intact person who'll be awake for all five years: dignity/autonomy, food (eat a meal there), real activities for a non-ambulatory sharp mind, the ability to actually get outdoors, roommate/privacy, and staff continuity.
  6. Decide on convergence — when a great star rating conflicts with what current families and your own evening visit tell you, trust the people and the visit.
