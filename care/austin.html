Austin-metro nursing facilities — best options (private resources available)

For an elderly, cognitively-intact, bariatric resident who recently lost the ability to walk, needs heavy-care nursing + a rehab goal (rebuild strength to transfer / walk to the bathroom), and may stay multiple years. Medicaid is no longer a filter — ranked by quality; Medicaid acceptance shown as an info column. Coverage: Austin + suburbs (Travis/Williamson/Hays). CMS data ~May 2026; verify live before acting.

What dropping the Medicaid requirement actually changes: Most of Austin's best homes already accept Medicaid, so private pay doesn't reveal a hidden tier — but it (1) adds Querencia at Barton Creek, an entrance-fee CCRC with the metro's highest RN staffing; (2) lets you choose a private room and skip Medicaid-bed waitlists at the dual-certified places; and (3) opens the CCRC environments (Longhorn Village, The Arbour) that suit a multi-year stay. The Converts to Medicaid? column still matters: for a years-long stay, a facility that takes private pay now and Medicaid later means you never have to move her if funds run down.

For this patient specifically, weight these columns

  • Staffing & RN hours — heavy-care transfers and repositioning need hands; this is the single best predictor of good outcomes. (Note a CMS quirk: the staffing star is case-mix-adjusted, so a place with high raw nurse hours can still show a low star — read the raw "nurse hrs/day" too.)
  • Pressure ulcers % and falls-with-injury % — the two failure modes for a newly-immobile bariatric resident. Below ~8% (ulcers) and ~3% (falls) is good; above is a red flag for her in particular.
  • Rehab continuity — in-house (not contracted) therapy and a named restorative-nursing program (CNAs continuing the walking routine after Medicare therapy ends) decide whether the bathroom-walking goal survives past month two.
  • Bariatric equipment — in no database; confirm by phone for every candidate (bed/lift/wheelchair weight ratings, doorway/bath clearances). Only one metro facility advertises it (see the bariatric section), and that one has a poor safety record — so plan to verify equipment at a high-quality home instead.

Where to read high-quality reviews — federal + Texas sources

Same hierarchy: government data tells you which homes are safe; consumer reviews give texture but are gameable; bariatric + rehab capability is in no database. Use all three, in that order.

Federal (use first)

  • Medicare Care Compare — medicare.gov/care-compare. Read the three component stars separately and, for her, both short-stay rehab measures (improvement in function) and long-stay measures (pressure ulcers, falls, weight loss). Check Special-Focus status.
  • ProPublica Nursing Home Inspect — projects.propublica.org/nursing-homes. Searchable inspection narratives, fines, serious deficiencies.
  • US News Best Nursing Homes — health.usnews.com/best-nursing-homes. Rates Short-Term Rehab and Long-Term Care separately — useful here. (Cedar Pointe ranks #1 locally.)

Texas-specific (state-licensure detail)

  • Texas HHS LTC Provider Search — apps.hhs.texas.gov/ltcsearch — violations grouped Health vs Life-Safety, with correction dates.
  • TULIP licensure portal — tulip.hhs.texas.gov — full inspection reports, complaints, enforcement, no login.
  • There is no separate Texas consumer star rating — Texas defers to the federal CMS Five-Star. Complaint & Incident Intake: 1-800-458-9858.

Austin local help — free and conflict-free

  • Capital Area LTC Ombudsman (AAACAP / CAPCOG) — resident-side advocate, no financial stake. 512-916-6054 (or 888-622-9111 opt. 3). Covers Travis, Williamson, Hays. Call before placing to ask which homes generate complaints and which actually have bariatric lift equipment.
  • Area Agency on Aging of the Capital Area — 512-916-6062 / 888-622-9111. Free benefits counseling (incl. the Medicaid pathway if funds later run down). capcog.org
  • 2-1-1 Texas — dial 2-1-1, 24/7.

Consumer reviews — texture only

Google Maps highest volume; read the 3-star reviews and look for recurring specifics. Avoid letting "A Place for Mom" / SeniorAdvisor / Caring "advisors" narrow your list — paid-referral channels that only show homes that pay them. Nursing-home star averages are unreliable (tiny samples, a barbell of grieving families and staff-solicited 5-stars); triangulate inspection narrative + staffing data + what current long-term families and the ombudsman tell you.

Phone screen — ask every candidate (rehab + bariatric + private-pay)

  1. "She recently became unable to walk. Realistically, toward transfers and walking to the bathroom — PT/OT how many days/week, in-house or contracted?"
  2. "After skilled therapy ends, is there a restorative-nursing program that continues a daily walking routine? Who runs it, how often?"
  3. "Do you currently care for bariatric residents over [weight]? How many now?"
  4. "Rated weight capacity — in pounds — of beds, ceiling/floor lifts, slings, wheelchairs, shower chairs, and the rehab gym equipment?" (beds ≈ 1.5× her weight)
  5. "How many trained staff per bariatric transfer, on every shift incl. nights/weekends?"
  6. "Can the bathroom/shower fit a bariatric shower chair + a lift?"
  7. "Pressure-injury prevention — repositioning schedule, mattress type + weight rating, wound-care staffing?"
  8. "We can pay privately. What's the all-in daily/monthly private rate, can we get a private room, and — if funds eventually run down — does this bed convert to Medicaid without a move?" (Critical for the CCRCs.)
  9. "Any bariatric-handling injuries (resident or staff) in the past year?"

Then visit twice — once unannounced on an evening or weekend — and ask current long-term families in the hallway what they'd change.
