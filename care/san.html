San Antonio-metro nursing facilities — best options (private resources available)

For an elderly, cognitively-intact, bariatric resident who recently lost the ability to walk, needs heavy-care nursing + a rehab goal (rebuild strength to transfer / walk to the bathroom), and may stay multiple years. Ranked by quality; Medicaid acceptance is an info column, not a filter. Coverage: Bexar County + suburbs. CMS data queried 2026-06-14; verify live before acting.

San Antonio is a much deeper bench than the Kerrville area — six 5-star and eight 4-star certified nursing facilities, versus two viable options near Kerrville. It's ~65 mi SE of Kerrville (closer than Austin). Dropping the Medicaid filter here adds two premium private-pay CCRCs (Blue Skies / The Mission, and Army Residence) with the best safety numbers in the metro — but both have access caveats (military heritage + CCRC admission model). Most of the strong standalone homes already take Medicaid, so going private-pay mainly buys you a private room, no Medicaid-bed waitlist, and the premium environments.

For this patient, weight these columns

  • Pressure ulcers % and falls-with-injury % — the two failure modes for a newly-immobile bariatric resident. Below ~8% (ulcers) and ~3% (falls) is good. This is the single most decision-relevant clinical data here.
  • Staffing & RN hours — heavy-care transfers need hands. (The CMS staffing star is case-mix-adjusted, so also read the raw "nurse hrs/day.")
  • Rehab continuity — in-house (not contracted) therapy + a named restorative-nursing program decide whether the bathroom-walking goal survives past month two.
  • Bariatric equipment — in no database, and not a single San Antonio SNF advertises it. Confirm by phone for every candidate (bed/lift/wheelchair weight ratings, doorway/bath clearances). See the bariatric note below for a different route for the intensive rehab phase.

The bariatric problem — and a different route for the rehab phase

No San Antonio SNF advertises bariatric capability (checked across Touchstone, Ensign, Cantex, Regent, and the faith-based homes). So for any choice, the admissions/DON must confirm three things by phone: (1) maximum bed weight rating, (2) lift type and pound capacity for dependent transfers, (3) a room sized for a bariatric bed plus lift clearance.

One option worth considering for the intensive rehab phase specifically: an inpatient rehabilitation hospital / LTAC (e.g., PAM Health Warm Springs has San Antonio locations) is a different, higher level of care than a SNF, delivers ~3 hours/day of therapy, and these facilities more often stock heavy-capacity equipment. A plausible path: a short intensive bariatric-rehab admission at a rehab hospital to push the walking goal, then transition to one of the Tier-A SNFs above for the long-term custodial stay. Ask her physician and the rehab hospital's admissions team whether she qualifies for inpatient rehab first.

Where to read high-quality reviews — federal + Texas + San Antonio sources

Federal (use first)

  • Medicare Care Compare — medicare.gov/care-compare. Read the three component stars separately and, for her, both short-stay (function improvement) and long-stay (pressure ulcers, falls, weight loss) measures. Check Special-Focus status.
  • ProPublica Nursing Home Inspect — projects.propublica.org/nursing-homes — searchable inspection narratives, fines, serious deficiencies. (Bexar County: c-130.)
  • US News Best Nursing Homes — San Antonio listings — rates Short-Term Rehab and Long-Term Care separately.

Texas-specific

  • Texas HHS LTC Provider Search — apps.hhs.texas.gov/ltcsearch — state-licensure violations (Health vs Life-Safety) with correction dates.
  • TULIP licensure portal — tulip.hhs.texas.gov — full inspection reports, complaints, enforcement; no login. (There's no separate Texas star rating — Texas defers to CMS.)
  • Complaint & Incident Intake (report a facility): 1-800-458-9858.

San Antonio local help — free and conflict-free

  • Bexar County LTC Ombudsman (AACOG) — resident-side advocate, no financial stake: 210-362-5236. Call before placing to ask which homes generate complaints and which actually have bariatric lift equipment.
  • Bexar Area Agency on Aging (AACOG) — 210-477-3275; Alamo Service Connection / ADRC intake: 855-937-2372 (askasc.org). Free benefits counseling, incl. the Medicaid/STAR+PLUS pathway if funds later run down. aacog.gov
  • 2-1-1 Texas — dial 2-1-1, 24/7.

Consumer reviews — texture only

Google Maps highest volume (The Heights on Huebner's 4.5/388 is unusually strong and well-sourced); read 3-star reviews for honesty. Avoid letting "A Place for Mom" / SeniorAdvisor / Caring "advisors" narrow your list — paid-referral channels. Triangulate inspection narrative + staffing data + what current long-term families and the ombudsman tell you.

Phone screen — ask every candidate (rehab + bariatric + private-pay)

  1. "She recently became unable to walk. Toward transfers and walking to the bathroom — PT/OT how many days/week, in-house or contracted?"
  2. "After skilled therapy ends, is there a restorative-nursing program that continues a daily walking routine? Who runs it, how often?"
  3. "Do you currently care for bariatric residents over [weight]? How many now?"
  4. "Rated weight capacity — in pounds — of beds, ceiling/floor lifts, slings, wheelchairs, shower chairs, and the rehab gym equipment?" (beds ≈ 1.5× her weight)
  5. "How many trained staff per bariatric transfer, on every shift incl. nights/weekends?"
  6. "Can the bathroom/shower fit a bariatric shower chair + a lift, and is the room sized for a bariatric bed plus lift clearance?"
  7. "Pressure-injury prevention — repositioning schedule, mattress type + weight rating, wound-care staffing?"
  8. "We can pay privately. All-in daily/monthly private rate? Can we get a private room? If funds run down, does this bed convert to Medicaid without a move?" (For the CCRCs, also: eligibility restrictions, and do you accept outside direct admits?)
  9. "Any bariatric-handling injuries (resident or staff) in the past year?"

Then visit twice — once unannounced on an evening or weekend — and ask current long-term families in the hallway what they'd change.
